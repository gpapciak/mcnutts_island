Deep Time

Ancient Ground

The granite underfoot on McNutt's Island has a longer story than most people imagine. It began not here, but near what is now northwest Africa, roughly 500 million years ago, when the rocks that would become Nova Scotia's South Shore were forming as deep-sea sediment on the margin of the ancient supercontinent Gondwana.

Southern Nova Scotia belongs to what geologists call the Meguma terrane — a fragment of crust that separated from Gondwana and drifted northward over tens of millions of years before colliding with the ancient continent of Euramerica around 390 million years ago. That collision built mountains, metamorphosed ancient seafloor sediments into slate and quartzite, and drove enormous plumes of magma up into the crust. As that magma cooled slowly underground, it crystallized into the granite now exposed across the South Shore — including every headland, boulder field, and rocky beach on this island.

The pale grey stone beneath your feet, flecked with dark minerals, is around 370 million years old.

The coastline itself is far younger. As the Atlantic Ocean opened about 165 million years ago, the sea began to claim the continental margins. Then the glaciers came — kilometres-thick ice sheets that scoured and polished the bedrock, strewed it with erratic boulders, and left behind the thin, nutrient-poor soils that explain the island's sparse forest cover and open barrens. The ice retreated around 13,000 years ago. As it melted, sea levels rose, flooding the low valleys and passages around the island's granite ridges. McNutt's Island didn't rise from the sea; the sea rose around it, defining its shape and separating it from the mainland.

What remains is a landscape written in deep time: ancient rock, a thin skin of glacial soil, the open Atlantic, and a spruce forest doing what it can with both.

Ten Thousand Years

Mi'kmaq Homeland

Long before European contact, Mi'kmaq people inhabited and traveled through the territory surrounding Shelburne Harbour, which they called Ounemegeek — a name for the shelter the deep harbour provided. The island and its surrounding waters were part of Mi'kma'ki, the ancestral and unceded territory of the Mi'kmaq Nation.

The Mi'kmaq maintained seasonal camps, fished the abundant harbour, and moved through a landscape they had known for millennia. The Sipekne'katik First Nation, whose traditional territory includes this region, maintains a living connection to these lands and waters.

McNutt's Island Alliance acknowledges this history and seeks to engage in genuine partnership with Mi'kmaq communities on questions of stewardship and the island's future — including the question of the island's name.

1783 – The Black Loyalists

Promised Land, Broken Promises

In 1783, following the American Revolutionary War, Black Loyalists — free Black people who had sided with the British in exchange for promises of freedom and land — arrived on Nova Scotia's South Shore. Nearby Birchtown became the largest free Black settlement in North American history at the time, with more than 3,000 residents.

Many of these settlers had connections to McNutt's Island and Shelburne Harbour. The promises made to them were largely broken: land grants were smaller than promised, the land itself often poor, and discrimination rampant. Within a decade, many had left for Sierra Leone aboard the "Province of Freedom" ships.

This history — one of the most significant and underrecognized chapters in both Canadian and African-American history — is woven into the fabric of McNutt's Island. The Black Loyalist Heritage Society in Shelburne preserves and continues this story.

Since 1788

The Lighthouse

The McNutt's Island lighthouse has guided vessels into Shelburne Harbour since 1788, making it one of the oldest lighthouses in Canada. Built originally of wood, the current octagonal wooden tower dates to 1833. It stands 21 metres tall on the island's southwestern headland, visible for miles in clear conditions.

The lighthouse was decommissioned in the 1980s and is now managed by the McNutt's Island Lighthouse Preservation Society. Restoration work has been ongoing, and the lighthouse is accessible to visitors who make the crossing from Gunning Cove.

The lighthouse keeper's dwelling and fog alarm building still stand nearby, offering a glimpse into the life of those who maintained this critical navigational aid through Nova Scotia's often brutal winter storms.

1939 – 1945

The WWII Gun Battery

During the Second World War, McNutt's Island was fortified as part of Canada's coastal defence network. A gun battery was installed at McNutt's Head to protect Shelburne Harbour — a strategic deep-water port used as a convoy assembly point for trans-Atlantic crossings.

The concrete battery emplacements remain visible today, partially reclaimed by the forest. The battery was armed with two six-inch naval guns capable of engaging surface vessels at considerable range. Supporting infrastructure included barracks, searchlight positions, and communications equipment.

The wartime period brought significant activity and population to the island — a striking contrast to its usual quiet, and a reminder that even remote places are not immune to the currents of world events.

The Working Island

The Lobster Fishery

McNutt's Island sits at the edge of some of the most productive lobster grounds on Nova Scotia's South Shore. The lobster fishery has been the economic backbone of the surrounding communities for generations — and it remains so today.

Fishing families maintain seasonal camps on the island, returning each December for the lobster season, which runs through to May. This working connection to the island is one of its most vital threads — a living relationship between people and place that has persisted through all the historical changes around it.

The health of the lobster fishery is tied to the health of the marine ecosystem: the eelgrass beds, the intertidal zones, the water quality of Shelburne Harbour. Environmental stewardship and the fishery are not in opposition — they depend on each other.
