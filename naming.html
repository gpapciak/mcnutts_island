A Community Conversation

The Naming

McNutt's Island — twenty-four hundred acres of spruce, granite, and open Atlantic — carries the name of a British colonial officer. Ten thousand years of Mi'kmaq presence in these waters, 240 years of Black Loyalist history, generations of lighthouse keepers, a wartime garrison, and a living fishing community also have a claim. This is the community's conversation to have.

A Long History

Key Dates

~10,000 years ago
Mi'kmaq presence begins in the region — ten thousand years of navigation, settlement, and stewardship in the waters around what would be called Shelburne Harbour.
October 1765
Alexander McNutt receives Port Roseway township grant — one parcel in a three-week frenzy of speculative land granting that distributed 3 million acres across Nova Scotia.
1768
The Port Roseway township, including the island, is sold to pay McNutt's debts. Most of his Nova Scotia grants are escheated for non-development.
1783
Black Loyalists arrive at Shelburne and Birchtown — the largest free Black settlement in North American history, with more than 3,000 residents. Many have connections to the harbour and island.
1788
Cape Roseway Lighthouse established — one of the earliest lighthouses in Canada. The island begins its long role as a navigational landmark for ships entering Shelburne Harbour.
Late 1780s–1794
McNutt lives intermittently on the island after returning from the American Revolution. He appears on Shelburne county tax lists in 1786, 1787, and 1794.
1796
McNutt leaves for Virginia permanently. He dies there around 1811, unmarried, in Lexington, Virginia — largely forgotten outside the Dictionary of Canadian Biography.
c. 1798
A map of the period records the island under the name Roseneath — a Scottish place name, distinct from McNutt's own. The name did not endure in common usage, but its presence on the historical record is a reminder that what we call this island has never been fixed.
1939–1945
WWII gun battery constructed at McNutt's Head for the defence of Shelburne Harbour — a strategic deep-water convoy assembly point. The concrete emplacements remain today.
Present
The naming conversation begins. The McNutt's Island Alliance creates space for the community — residents, property owners, Mi'kmaq voices, the fishing community — to engage with the question.
The Question

Why This Conversation Matters

Colonel Alexander McNutt received the island as part of a Port Roseway township grant in October 1765 — one parcel in a frenzy of speculative land granting that saw 3 million acres parcelled out across Nova Scotia in just three weeks. McNutt personally acquired the Port Roseway township and, with various associates, obtained grants totalling roughly half of all land distributed during that period. He was a relentless self-promoter and land speculator whose proposals consistently outran reality — and that name, blunt and possessive, has sat on this island for 260 years like an uncashed cheque.

McNutt (1725–c.1811) was born in Londonderry, Northern Ireland, and came to Nova Scotia as a colonizer and land agent. He made sweeping promises to recruit thousands of Irish settlers, chartered ships, and petitioned the Board of Trade repeatedly — but his schemes consistently collapsed. The Privy Council blocked his largest plans. Most of his grants were escheated for non-development. The Port Roseway township that included the island was sold in 1768 to pay his debts.

He did live on the island intermittently with his brother Benjamin in the late 1760s, and returned after the American Revolution, appearing on Shelburne tax lists in 1786, 1787, and 1794. He left for Virginia in 1796 and died there around 1811. His connection to the island was real — but largely defined by speculation and debt, not by the kind of sustained relationship with a place that earns the right to name it.

Historical maps show the island was also recorded under the name Roseneath — a Scottish place name that appears in period documents but never took hold in common usage. That a second name existed at all, so early in the colonial record, is itself telling: the island's identity was contested and unsettled from the start.

It is a name that tells you nothing about the place. Nothing about the spruce and granite, the open Atlantic, the seals and eagles. Nothing about the Mi'kmaq people who navigated these waters and coastlines for ten thousand years before any European arrived with a surveyor's map. Nothing about the Black Loyalist families who came in 1783 seeking freedom they were largely denied, and who built lives here anyway. Nothing about the lighthouse keepers, the wartime garrison, the fishing families whose boats still work these waters.

The McNutt's Island Alliance is not calling for an immediate name change. We are creating space for the community — area residents, property owners, researchers, the fishing community, Mi'kmaq voices and others touched by the island — to engage with the question seriously. An island of this history and beauty deserves a name that carries some of that weight, or at least doesn't actively misrepresent it. Whatever the community decides, the decision should be made with full awareness of the history.

Alexander McNutt
(1725–c.1811)

Born in Londonderry, Northern Ireland. Army officer, colonizer, and land speculator. Recruited Irish settlers for Nova Scotia with grand promises rarely fulfilled. Acquired massive land grants, most of which were escheated for non-development or sold to pay debts.

Lived intermittently on the island in the late 1760s with his brother Benjamin, and again after the American Revolution. Left for Virginia in 1796. Died unmarried around 1811 in Lexington, Virginia.

Source: Dictionary of Canadian Biography
