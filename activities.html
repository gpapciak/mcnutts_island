Visiting & Exploring

Activities on the Island

Hiking the lighthouse trail, kayaking the harbour, foraging wild berries, birding the headlands. McNutt's Island rewards those who make the crossing.

First Things First

Getting to the Island

McNutt's Island is accessible only by water. There is no public ferry service — visitors arrive by private boat, kayak, or chartered water taxi from the Gunning Cove boat launch, approximately 15 minutes from Shelburne town centre.

The crossing from Gunning Cove to Landing Cove is approximately 4.5 kilometres of sheltered harbour water — about 15 minutes by motorboat and 45–60 minutes by kayak. The closest point between mainland and island is roughly 1 km, but the route to the protected landing bay is longer. Conditions in Shelburne Harbour are generally calm, but weather changes quickly, particularly in spring and fall. Check the forecast and tide tables before crossing.

Gunning Cove has a public boat launch and limited parking. From Shelburne, take Highway 3 west, turn onto Gunning Cove Road, and follow to the water.

There are no services on McNutt's Island — no convenience store, no medical facilities. Come prepared with everything you need.

What to Do

Activities

Hiking

The lighthouse trail winds through Acadian forest and along the island's western shore to the 1788 lighthouse at McNutt's Head. The round trip is approximately 6 kilometres of moderate walking, with excellent views of the Atlantic and the harbour approaches.

The trail passes through bog sections — bring waterproof boots and expect wet ground. The WWII gun battery emplacements are a short side trail off the main route. The lighthouse grounds are open to visitors.

Kayaking

Sea kayaking around McNutt's Island is one of the finest paddles on the South Shore. The sheltered harbour side is suitable for beginners; the outer coast requires more experience and attention to weather.

Kayak rentals are available in Shelburne. Paddlers should be comfortable self-rescuing in cold water and aware of tidal currents at the island's points.

Foraging

The island's open areas produce an abundance of wild blueberries, raspberries, and wild strawberries in summer. Chanterelle mushrooms are found throughout the forest after rain. Beach pea and sea rocket are edible coastal plants.

Forage lightly and knowledgeably. Take only what you'll use; leave the rest for the animals and for next season. Never harvest from unfamiliar plants.

Birding

McNutt's Island is exceptional birding territory — bald eagles year-round, migratory songbirds in spring and fall, nesting osprey and great blue herons through summer, and wintering sea ducks offshore.

Bring binoculars and a field guide. Spring migration (late April–May) and fall migration (August–October) are the most productive times for warbler and shorebird diversity.

Visitor Information

Staying on the Island

There are no commercial accommodations on McNutt's Island. Camping is possible with appropriate preparation — you are responsible for carrying in everything you need and carrying out all waste. There are no facilities.

Several island residents and property owners provide seasonal accommodation; contact the McNutt's Island Alliance for referrals to properties that may be available for short-term rental.

Staying in Shelburne

The town of Shelburne, 15 minutes from Gunning Cove, offers a full range of accommodations from B&Bs to motels. Shelburne is a heritage town with excellent restaurants, the Shelburne County Museum, and the historic waterfront.

Visit Responsibly

McNutt's Island is a working landscape — fishing families depend on it, wildlife nests on it, and a fragile ecosystem depends on visitors who tread lightly. Leave no trace. Respect nesting areas. Stay on established paths where possible. Take only photographs.
